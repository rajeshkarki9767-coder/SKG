What Is Glycolic Acid and Why Does It Work So Well in Nepal?

Glycolic acid is an alpha hydroxy acid (AHA) derived from sugarcane. It is the smallest AHA molecule, meaning it penetrates skin most effectively of all AHAs. In skincare concentrations of 5–10%, it acts as a chemical exfoliant — dissolving the bonds between dead skin cells and accelerating their shedding, revealing fresh, brighter skin underneath.

In Nepal's context, glycolic acid addresses the two most common skin complaints simultaneously. First: hyperpigmentation and post-acne dark marks, which are highly prevalent in South Asian skin phototypes due to Nepal's UV intensity and monsoon-driven acne cycles. Second: dull, rough skin texture caused by slow cell turnover, which is worsened by pollution-related oxidative stress and the dry winter air that causes surface skin cells to accumulate rather than shed naturally.

How Glycolic Acid Works

Glycolic acid works by breaking the chemical bonds (desmosomes) that hold dead skin cells together in the outer layer of the skin. When these bonds dissolve, dead cells shed, revealing the newer, more evenly pigmented cells beneath. With consistent use, this accelerated cell turnover: reduces the visible depth of post-acne marks; improves overall skin tone evenness; smooths rough texture on cheeks and forehead; reduces the appearance of enlarged pores (pores look smaller when the surrounding skin is freshly exfoliated); and over time, stimulates fibroblasts to produce more collagen.

At concentrations above 10%, glycolic acid also stimulates glycosaminoglycan production (hyaluronic acid synthesis in the dermis), providing anti-ageing benefits beyond simple exfoliation. This is why products like The Ordinary Glycolic Acid 7% Toning Solution produce measurable skin plumpness improvements with consistent use over months.

How to Use Glycolic Acid in Nepal's Climate

Glycolic acid must be used correctly for Nepal's climate. Start with twice-weekly use on dry skin after cleansing. Apply at evening only — AHAs increase photosensitivity and Nepal's UV intensity makes morning AHA application genuinely risky. The next morning, SPF 50+ PA++++ is non-negotiable: the freshly exfoliated skin layer is particularly vulnerable to UV darkening, which would undo the brightening the acid achieved.

After 4 weeks of twice-weekly use with no irritation, increase to three times per week. Most Nepalis with oily or combination skin can tolerate this frequency year-round. During Nepal's dry winter months (December–February), reduce to twice-weekly maximum and always follow with a ceramide moisturiser — dry skin with a compromised barrier does not tolerate AHA exfoliation well.

Nepal monsoon tip: glycolic acid is more effective in humid conditions because the moisture aids penetration. Three-times weekly during monsoon produces faster results for dark spot reduction than the same frequency in winter.

Glycolic Acid vs Other AHAs for Nepal

Glycolic vs Lactic Acid: Lactic acid is gentler (larger molecule, less penetration) and better for dry or sensitive skin. For Nepal's dry winter, lactic acid (The Ordinary Lactic Acid 5% + HA, Rs. 1,200) is preferable to glycolic during the months of lowest humidity. Switch back to glycolic in monsoon when skin is better hydrated and can tolerate deeper exfoliation.

Glycolic vs Mandelic Acid: Mandelic acid is the gentlest AHA and specifically beneficial for post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation in darker skin tones (Fitzpatrick IV–V). Paula's Choice Clinical Discolouration Repair Serum contains mandelic acid and is available on Daraz — good option for very sensitive Nepali skin types.

Glycolic vs BHA (Salicylic Acid): BHA penetrates pores (oil-soluble); AHA exfoliates the surface. Both are needed for complete exfoliation in Nepal's pollution-heavy environment. Alternating use — BHA three nights per week, glycolic acid two other nights — provides comprehensive exfoliation without overlapping on the same night.

Common Glycolic Acid Mistakes in Nepal

Using it on damp or wet skin increases penetration unpredictably — wait for completely dry skin. Using it daily without building tolerance causes a damaged, sensitised barrier that worsens hyperpigmentation. Combining it with retinol on the same night causes excessive irritation — use on alternate nights. Skipping SPF the morning after use in Nepal's UV environment negates all brightening progress and can worsen pigmentation.

Expected Results Timeline for Nepal

Week 1–2: Mild skin flaking (normal — cells shedding faster). Week 3–4: Skin appears brighter and smoother. Month 2: Post-acne marks begin visibly fading. Month 3: Measurable reduction in dark spot depth. Month 4–6: Continued brightening and texture improvement. Results are cumulative — consistent use produces compound improvement over time.
