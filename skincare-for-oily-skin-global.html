Oily Skin Around the World: Why Climate Changes Everything

Oily skin is one of the most common skin types globally, affecting an estimated 40–55% of adults. But "oily skin" in Tokyo behaves differently from "oily skin" in Lagos, which is different again from oily skin in London or Kathmandu. The sebaceous glands that produce excess oil are influenced by temperature, humidity, diet, genetics, and hormones — all of which vary dramatically by region. This guide addresses oily skin care by region, climate, and skin tone.

Nepal and South Asia: Monsoon Oiliness

Nepali oily skin faces the world's most dramatic seasonal swings. In monsoon (June–September), Kathmandu's humidity exceeds 85% and temperatures reach 30–35°C — creating ideal conditions for sebum overproduction. The skin responds to heat by increasing oil production as a cooling mechanism, and high humidity prevents evaporation, leaving oil visibly pooled on the surface.

Best approach for Nepal's monsoon season: gel-based cleansers (COSRX Low pH Good Morning, Rs. 1,600), lightweight niacinamide serums (The Ordinary 10% Niacinamide, Rs. 1,100), and water-gel moisturisers (Neutrogena Hydro Boost, Rs. 1,600). SPF 50+ PA++++ in a non-greasy formula like ISNTREE Watery Sun Gel (Rs. 2,000) is essential given Nepal's extreme UV. Avoid heavy creams entirely during monsoon.

Nepal's winter (December–February) presents the opposite challenge: dry air at 30–40% humidity causes the skin to produce more oil as a compensatory mechanism. The skin feels oily but is actually dehydrated. Introducing a lightweight hyaluronic acid serum beneath a gel moisturiser paradoxically reduces oiliness by providing the moisture the skin needs.

East Asia: Korea, Japan, China — The Innovation Centres

East Asian markets — Korea, Japan, and China — lead global skincare innovation for oily skin, driven by high consumer demand and sophisticated formulation science. Japanese oily skin culture is built around "sebum control" — the concept of managing oil without stripping. Products like SK-II Facial Treatment Essence and Hada Labo Gokujyun products focus on layering lightweight humectants. Korean formulations took this further with BHA-containing toners, snail mucin, and centella asiatica extracts that address both oiliness and the post-acne marks common in East Asian oily skin types.

Korea's climate — hot, humid summers (70–80% humidity in Seoul) and dry winters — is actually quite similar to Kathmandu's seasonal pattern, making Korean skincare particularly relevant for Nepali consumers. The COSRX product range was specifically developed for Korean oily skin and translates excellently to South Asian oily skin types.

China's diverse climate means regional variations: southern China (Guangzhou, Shenzhen) has tropical-humid conditions year-round, demanding lightweight oil-control formulas similar to Nepal's monsoon approach. Northern China (Beijing) has dry continental winters requiring the hydration-first oily skin approach.

Southeast Asia: Thailand, Vietnam, Philippines, Indonesia

Southeast Asian countries face year-round tropical conditions — high heat (28–35°C), high humidity (75–90%), and intense UV radiation. Oily skin in Bangkok, Manila, or Jakarta is consistently more active than in temperate climates. The challenge is managing oiliness without dehydrating the skin, which triggers even more sebum production.

Regional favourites include: rice water (a traditional Southeast Asian remedy with mild oil-balancing properties), centella asiatica (extremely popular in Korean products now but native to the region), and tamarind-based products with natural AHA properties. Modern K-beauty has largely displaced traditional approaches in urban Southeast Asia, with COSRX, Some By Mi, and Innisfree dominating skincare counters in Bangkok, Ho Chi Minh City, and Manila.

Key ingredients for Southeast Asian tropical oily skin: niacinamide 10%, salicylic acid BHA, centella asiatica, green tea extract (natural antioxidant and mild sebum reducer), and clay masks (kaolin or bentonite clay) used weekly to deep-clean pores overwhelmed by year-round heat and humidity.

India: Diverse Climate, Shared Skin Type

India's vast size means oily skin management varies significantly: Mumbai's coastal humidity year-round, Rajasthan's extreme dry heat, Kerala's tropical conditions, and Himalayan states' altitude-driven UV. Indian oily skin tends toward Fitzpatrick types IV–V with a strong predisposition to post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation — meaning acne from oily skin leaves darker marks than in lighter skin types.

Indian skincare market has undergone rapid premiumisation. Products like Minimalist (an Indian brand similar to The Ordinary) at 10% niacinamide for Rs. 599, Dot & Key Cica and Green Tea range, and mamaearth's niacinamide products have made evidence-based oily skin care accessible at Indian price points. Traditional remedies like multani mitti (Fuller's Earth clay) used as a weekly mask remain effective and widely used.

Middle East: High UV, Dry Heat, and Oil Control

Gulf countries — Saudi Arabia, UAE, Kuwait — present a unique oily skin challenge: extreme heat (40–50°C outdoors in summer), very low humidity (10–20%), and high UV. The dry air reduces visible shininess but oily skin continues producing sebum as an internal response to heat. This "hidden oiliness" leads to congested pores even when the skin surface appears matte. Heavy makeup culture in the Gulf — full-coverage foundation in extreme heat — dramatically worsens comedonal acne in oily skin types.

Middle Eastern oily skin benefits from: micellar waters for gentle oil removal, lightweight oil-free SPF formulas (essential given the UV level), and BHA exfoliants used twice weekly to clear congestion caused by the dry-heat-plus-heavy-makeup combination. French pharmacy brands (La Roche-Posay, Vichy, Avène) dominate premium skincare in the Gulf, with their effaclar range specifically targeting oily and acne-prone skin.

Africa: Diverse Climates, Darker Skin Tones

Africa's skincare market is the world's fastest-growing, driven by a young population and rising income. Oily skin is extremely common across Sub-Saharan Africa, compounded by tropical and equatorial climates (Nigeria, Ghana, DRC, Kenya coastal regions) and darker skin phototypes (Fitzpatrick V–VI) that are highly prone to post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation from acne.

West Africa (Nigeria, Ghana, Côte d'Ivoire): Year-round heat and humidity (Lagos averages 26°C and 75–85% humidity) creates conditions for persistent oiliness. Nigerian skincare market has grown rapidly — local brands like Flawless by SK and international brands have expanded distribution. Key concerns: acne, dark spots, uneven tone, and the damage from skin-lightening products (bleaching) which remain problematic in the region despite health campaigns.

East Africa (Kenya, Ethiopia, Tanzania): More varied climate. Nairobi's altitude (1,800m — higher than Kathmandu) means significant UV radiation alongside moderate temperatures. Oily skin at altitude behaves differently: the UV exposure is higher, causing more oxidative damage to the sebaceous glands and more frequent post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation from any acne. Lightweight SPF is critically important but historically underused.

Across Africa, niacinamide has emerged as the key oily skin ingredient due to its dual action on sebum production and hyperpigmentation — addressing both the primary concern (oiliness and acne) and the secondary concern (dark spots) simultaneously. The Ordinary's Niacinamide 10% has seen significant market growth in major African cities through e-commerce.

Europe: Seasonal Oiliness and Cold-Climate Challenges

European oily skin is dominated by seasonal patterns. In summer — particularly Southern Europe (Spain, Italy, Greece) — hot, dry heat can reach 35–40°C with high UV, triggering sebum overproduction. In Central and Northern Europe (Germany, Scandinavia, UK), cold winters with central heating create dehydrated-oily conditions where the skin appears oily but lacks moisture in the deeper layers.

European skincare regulation is the world's strictest — over 1,300 ingredients banned versus 11 in the US. French pharmacy brands (La Roche-Posay Effaclar range, Vichy Normaderm, Avène Cleanance) dominate European oily skin care and are well-formulated for European climate conditions. Effaclar DUO+ and Effaclar MAT are considered gold-standard oily skin products in Europe.

UK oily skin faces London's combination of high humidity in summer and central heating-induced dryness in winter — plus significant air pollution. The Ordinary (founded in Canada, hugely popular in UK) has democratised ingredient-led skincare for UK consumers with its range of niacinamide, BHA, and vitamin C products at accessible price points.

Americas: North America's Ingredient Science vs Latin America's Melanin Concerns

United States oily skin care is led by ingredient science and dermatologist brands. CeraVe, Cetaphil, and La Roche-Posay dominate drugstore sales. Paula's Choice, The Ordinary, and Korean brands (COSRX on Amazon) lead the skincare-aware demographic. American oily skin routines tend toward minimalism — a trend driven by dermatologists countering the over-complicated 10-step routines promoted on social media.

Brazil, Mexico, and Colombia represent Latin America's largest skincare markets. Latin American oily skin shares the pigmentation concerns of South Asian skin (Fitzpatrick types III–V predominantly), making dark spot treatment alongside oil control the primary dual concern. Brazilian skincare culture is heavily influenced by dermatology clinics, with professional treatments (chemical peels, microneedling) widely accepted alongside home care.

Universal Oily Skin Ingredients That Work Globally

Regardless of climate or region, these ingredients consistently produce the best results for oily skin:

Niacinamide 5–10%: Reduces sebum production by up to 30% in 8 weeks. Non-irritating, safe for all skin tones. The highest-return single ingredient for oily skin globally.

Salicylic Acid 0.5–2% (BHA): Oil-soluble, penetrates pores, dissolves sebum plugs. Essential for blackheads and comedonal acne in any climate.

Zinc (PCA Zinc or Zinc Sulphate): Regulates sebaceous gland activity. The Ordinary Niacinamide 10% + Zinc 1% combines both in one product.

Clay (Kaolin or Bentonite): Physically absorbs surface oil. Used as a weekly mask rather than daily, to avoid over-drying.

AHAs (Glycolic or Lactic Acid): Remove dead skin cells that combine with sebum to cause congestion. Use 2–3 times per week in the evening.

The Most Common Oily Skin Mistakes — Universal

Whether you are in Kathmandu, Lagos, London, or Seoul, the mistakes are the same: over-cleansing (strips barrier, causes rebound oiliness), skipping moisturiser (skin produces more oil when dehydrated), using alcohol-based toners (identical effect to over-cleansing), and skipping SPF (UV damage stimulates sebum production). The solution is identical everywhere: gentle cleansing, lightweight hydration, SPF daily, and targeted actives (niacinamide + BHA) for oil regulation.

Conclusion

Oily skin is managed differently across climates, but the underlying biology is the same everywhere. The best global oily skin routine is consistent: pH-balanced cleanser, niacinamide serum, lightweight moisturiser, SPF 50+ — morning and evening.
