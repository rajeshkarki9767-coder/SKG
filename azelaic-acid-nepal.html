Azelaic Acid: The Most Overlooked Skincare Ingredient for Nepal

While niacinamide, retinol, and vitamin C dominate Nepal's skincare conversation, azelaic acid quietly addresses four of Nepal's most common skin concerns simultaneously: acne, post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH), rosacea, and dull uneven skin tone. It is the only ingredient with strong clinical evidence for all four — and it does so without the photosensitivity concerns of retinol or the stability issues of vitamin C.

Azelaic acid is a naturally occurring dicarboxylic acid originally isolated from rye. At 10–15% concentration in OTC products (and up to 20% in prescription formulations), it inhibits tyrosinase (reducing melanin production), kills the P. acnes bacteria responsible for acne, reduces abnormal keratin formation in pores, and has anti-inflammatory properties that reduce rosacea-related redness.

Why Azelaic Acid Is Particularly Valuable in Nepal

Nepal's high UV environment means post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation is more severe and longer-lasting than in lower-altitude countries. Azelaic acid addresses this in two ways: by reducing melanin production (preventing new pigmentation) and by inhibiting the inflammatory cascade that causes PIH in the first place. This dual action is particularly relevant for Nepal's combination of acne-prone skin plus UV-driven pigmentation.

For Nepali women dealing with melasma — hormonally-driven facial pigmentation worsened by Nepal's UV — azelaic acid is one of very few ingredients proven effective that is also safe during pregnancy. Unlike hydroquinone (avoidable in pregnancy), retinol (contraindicated), and strong AHAs, azelaic acid has an excellent safety profile for pregnant Nepali women managing melasma.

How to Use Azelaic Acid in Nepal

Apply once daily initially (evening) for the first two weeks to assess tolerance. Azelaic acid causes mild tingling and temporary redness in some users — this is normal and subsides within 15–20 minutes. If tingling persists beyond 30 minutes, dilute by mixing with your moisturiser before applying.

After two weeks with good tolerance, increase to twice daily (morning and evening). Unlike retinol, azelaic acid does not cause significant photosensitivity — morning use is safe in Nepal's climate with SPF 50+. SPF remains essential as always, but the photosensitivity risk is lower than retinol or AHAs.

Apply after cleansing and toning, before moisturiser. Do not layer with high-concentration vitamin C on the same application — both are brightening acids and the combination can be irritating. Use vitamin C in the morning and azelaic acid in the evening, or alternate mornings.

Expected Results in Nepal's Climate

Acne improvement: visible reduction in inflammatory acne within 4–6 weeks. Post-acne marks: measurable fading within 8–10 weeks. Rosacea redness: improvement over 3–6 months of consistent use. Melasma: significant improvement over 6–12 months (melasma requires the longest treatment duration of any pigmentation concern). Unlike niacinamide which works quickly and then plateaus, azelaic acid's results continue improving with months of consistent use.

Azelaic Acid vs Alternatives for Nepal's Concerns

For post-acne marks specifically, the combination of niacinamide 10% (reducing melanin transfer) + azelaic acid 10% (inhibiting tyrosinase + anti-inflammation) is the most effective OTC depigmentation stack available in Nepal, addressing complementary mechanisms simultaneously. This combination is available through Daraz for approximately Rs. 2,600–3,200 for both products together.
