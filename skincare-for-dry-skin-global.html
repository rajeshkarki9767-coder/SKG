Skincare for Dry Skin Around the World — Global Guide 2026

10 min read·Global skincare·Updated May 2026
Advertisement

Why Dry Skin Is a Global Problem — and Why Solutions Differ

Dry skin — characterised by insufficient sebum production, a compromised lipid barrier, and trans-epidermal water loss (TEWL) — affects approximately 30% of adults globally. But its causes and the appropriate treatments vary dramatically by geography. Desert climates, cold continental winters, tropical monsoons at altitude, and year-round air conditioning all damage the skin barrier through different mechanisms. This guide addresses dry skin regionally, with specific product and ingredient recommendations for each climate zone.

Nepal and the Himalayas: Altitude + Cold Wind + Low Humidity

Nepal's dry skin challenge is severe and underappreciated. In Kathmandu (elevation 1,400m), winter brings the triple threat: temperature drops to 2–8°C, humidity falls to 30–40%, and cold mountain winds strip moisture continuously. At higher elevations — Mustang (3,800m), Everest Base Camp (5,364m) — humidity can drop below 10%, creating conditions where even well-moisturised skin develops visible cracks within hours of exposure.

The Nepali cultural practice of hot showers compounds the problem — hot water dissolves the lipid bilayer of the skin barrier within minutes. Traditional remedies have cultural wisdom: mustard oil (sarso ko tel) used as a pre-bath oil in Nepali households is actually an excellent occlusive that prevents water loss during bathing. Ghee applied to dry patches — a grandmother's remedy — is effective as an emollient-occlusive combination.

For modern Nepal: CeraVe Moisturising Cream (Rs. 1,900 at Bhatbhateni) contains three ceramides that rebuild the lipid barrier. The Ordinary Squalane (Rs. 1,200 on Daraz) applied before moisturiser adds a lipid layer similar to the skin's natural sebum. A humidifier targeting 45–55% indoor humidity is the single most impactful non-topical intervention for Nepal's dry skin during winter.

Scandinavia and Northern Europe: Cold Winters, Central Heating

Scandinavia (Norway, Sweden, Finland, Denmark) faces the world's most extreme seasonal skin barrier stress. Outdoor temperatures from November to March regularly drop to -10°C to -20°C in northern regions, while indoor central heating reduces humidity to 20–30% — similar to desert conditions. This combination strips the skin barrier both outdoors (cold, dry air) and indoors (heated air removing moisture).

Scandinavian skincare philosophy — minimalist, functional, focused on barrier repair — is well-adapted to these conditions. Brands like DECIEM/The Ordinary, Weleda, and Norwegian pharmacy brands (Apotek brands with ceramide bases) prioritise barrier-repair ingredients over complex multi-step routines. The concept of "skin fasting" (temporary simplification to reduce barrier stress) originated in Scandinavia.

For Northern European dry skin: ceramide creams (CeraVe, La Roche-Posay Lipikar) used generously morning and evening; facial oils (marula oil, rosehip oil — both abundant in Scandinavian natural product ranges) over moisturiser in winter; and the absolute avoidance of sodium lauryl sulfate (SLS) in cleansers, which is particularly damaging in cold conditions.

UK and Ireland: Damp Cold Without the Extreme

British and Irish climates are temperate — rarely extremely cold, but consistently damp, grey, and wind-exposed. London's average winter temperature is 4–8°C with 75–80% outdoor humidity — conditions that paradoxically cause barrier damage through wind and cold despite the relatively high humidity. Central heating in British homes, combined with the cultural preference for hot baths, creates indoor dryness that exacerbates outdoor exposure.

UK dermatology consensus for dry skin: Aveeno Moisturising Lotion (containing colloidal oatmeal), CeraVe Moisturising Cream, and E45 Cream are NHS-recommended options widely available at Boots and Superdrug. The UK market has also embraced the Korean ceramide revolution — Dr. Jart Ceramidin and Beauty of Joseon Dynasty Cream are among the best-selling facial moisturisers in UK K-beauty retail.

Russia and Eastern Europe: Extreme Continental Dry Cold

Moscow, Siberia, and Eastern European cities experience some of the world's harshest dry skin conditions: temperatures to -25°C to -40°C, outdoor humidity below 10%, and strong continental winds. Russian skincare culture has historically relied on heavy cream formulations — rich balms, petroleum-based products, and animal-fat-based creams — that are genuinely effective barrier repair tools in these conditions.

Russian pharmacy brands (Nivea in Russia has heavier formulations than Western versions), Burt's Bees, and the Eastern European tradition of rosehip oil and sea buckthorn oil are all well-adapted to cold dry skin. The key principle for extreme cold dry skin: layer multiple thin layers of occlusive products rather than one heavy layer — the skin absorbs layered applications more effectively.

Australia and New Zealand: Dry Heat + UV

Australian dry skin faces a unique challenge: intense UV radiation (UV index 10–14 in summer, among the world's highest) combined with dry heat in inland regions and moderate humidity on the coast. UV radiation damages the skin barrier by triggering oxidative stress and degrading the ceramide-lipid matrix — a process separate from moisture loss but equally damaging. Australian dry skin therefore requires both barrier repair AND antioxidant/SPF protection to be effective.

Australian dermatology brands (Ego QV range, Hamilton Skin Therapy) are globally underrated for their effective ceramide-based moisturisers designed for high-UV, moderate-climate conditions. The Aesop brand (Australian) has become globally recognised for sophisticated barrier-repair formulations. Cancer Council's SPF 50+ with moisturising formulas addresses the UV-plus-dryness dual challenge specific to Australia.

Sub-Saharan Africa: Harmattan Winds and Dry Season

West Africa's Harmattan season (November–March) brings dry, dusty winds from the Sahara that dramatically reduce humidity and deposit particulate matter on the skin. In Lagos, Accra, and Abidjan, humidity during Harmattan can drop from 75% to 20–30% within weeks — causing sudden-onset severe dryness in skin types that are normally oily. This seasonal reversal is one of the most dramatic climate-skin interactions anywhere in the world.

West African traditional dry skin remedies include shea butter (karité) — one of the world's most effective natural emollients and occlusives. Shea butter forms the base of many international moisturisers (L'Oreal, Dove, Cetaphil) precisely because of its efficacy, and the raw unrefined version from Burkina Faso or Ghana is as effective as most premium moisturisers at a fraction of the cost. Coconut oil (widely available across coastal Africa) provides additional lipid support.

For East Africa (Kenya, Ethiopia, Rwanda): Nairobi's altitude (1,800m) creates UV-related barrier damage similar to Kathmandu's. The combination of altitude UV and the dry season (July–September in much of East Africa) creates dry-plus-UV conditions requiring ceramide moisturisers plus SPF protection simultaneously.

Middle East and North Africa: Desert Dry Skin

Egypt, Morocco, and the broader MENA region face persistent dry skin from desert conditions: hot, dry air with humidity as low as 10–15%, combined with intense UV radiation and sandstorm particulates that physically abrade and dry the skin surface. Air conditioning — used extensively in Gulf countries — reduces indoor humidity to 20–30%, creating an indoor environment as dry as outdoor desert conditions.

Middle Eastern skincare has a tradition of argan oil (Morocco) — a non-comedogenic oil rich in oleic acid and vitamin E that is highly effective for dry skin in desert conditions. Modern Moroccan argan oil production supplies global luxury skincare, but local grades are available inexpensively throughout North Africa and the Levant. French dermocosmetic brands (La Roche-Posay Lipikar, Avène XeraCalm) are widely distributed in the region and well-formulated for desert conditions.

Latin America: Tropical Altitude Dry Skin

The Andes region — Bogotá, Colombia (2,600m elevation), Quito, Ecuador (2,850m), La Paz, Bolivia (3,640m) — creates dry skin conditions similar to Nepal's but at even higher altitudes. Bogotá's altitude combined with cool temperatures (average 14°C year-round) and periodic UV index peaks creates a cold-altitude UV combination that damages the skin barrier while simultaneously accelerating pigmentation in any unprotected areas.

Brazilian humid coast (São Paulo, Rio) presents the opposite: year-round humidity of 70–80% means dry skin is less common but dehydration (inadequate water-binding capacity) is prevalent. Hyaluronic acid serums are more appropriate than heavy ceramide creams in this climate. Neutrogena's Hydro Boost range has become Brazil's best-selling skincare line precisely because it addresses dehydration without the heaviness inappropriate for tropical conditions.

Universal Dry Skin Ingredients That Work in Every Climate

Ceramides (NP, AP, EOP): The backbone of the skin barrier's lipid matrix. CeraVe Moisturising Cream contains all three in ratios that mimic the skin's natural barrier. Available globally.

Glycerin: The most effective and affordable humectant. Draws water from the environment and deeper skin layers to the surface. Available at pharmacies in every country for minimal cost.

Hyaluronic Acid: Holds up to 1,000x its weight in water. Apply to damp skin for maximum effect. Universally available.

Squalane: A lightweight, non-comedogenic oil that mimics the skin's natural sebum. Derived from sugarcane or olives. Effective in every climate for lipid replenishment without greasiness.

Urea (5–10%): A keratolytic humectant that also exfoliates lightly. Particularly effective for very dry, thickened skin. Widely available in European pharmacy brands. Underused in Asia and the Americas.

The Universal Dry Skin Rule

Whatever your climate: apply moisturiser to damp skin within 3 minutes of cleansing. This "three-minute rule" is the single most impactful dry skin technique, applicable in Kathmandu, Lagos, London, or Sydney. Damp skin allows humectants to bind water to the surface rather than pulling it from deeper layers, and occlusives applied over damp skin create a more effective barrier seal than on dry skin.
